COURSE DESCRIPTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

7.016 Introductory Biology provides an introduction to fundamental principles
of biochemistry, molecular biology, and genetics for understanding the functions
of living systems. Taught for the first time in Fall 2013, this course covers
examples of the use of chemical biology and twenty-first century molecular
genetics in understanding human health and therapeutic intervention.

The course covers the same core material as other MIT introductory biology
courses (7.012, 7.013, 7.014, 7.015), which includes the fundamental principles
of biochemistry, genetics, molecular biology, and cell biology. Biological
function at the molecular level is particularly emphasized and covers the
structure and regulation of genes, as well as the structure and synthesis of
proteins, how these molecules are integrated into cells, and how these cells
are integrated into multicellular systems and organisms.

Topics covered include:
  - Biochemistry
  - Cell Biology
  - Genetics
  - Microbiology
  - Molecular Biology
  - Virology
  - Cancer
  - Functional Genomics

PREREQUISITES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

There are no formal prerequisites for this course. The course assumes
high-school-level biology and chemistry, especially familiarity with the
fundamental aspects of chemical structure. MIT offers a preparatory resource
called Pre-7.01 ("Getting Up to Speed in Biology") for students who need
additional preparation.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ASSIGNMENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Students complete six problem sets over the course of the semester. Only the
top five problem set scores count toward the final grade. Collaboration on
problem sets is permitted; however, detailed discussion of specific solutions
or sharing of answers is a violation of academic integrity. Copying solutions
results in zero credit for that assignment and may result in additional academic
integrity consequences.

A sixth special assignment—either a news brief or a hands-on project—
contributes 4% to the final grade. Options include:

  Option A – News Brief:
  Prepare a 1–2 page summary of an exciting recent development in the life
  sciences. The brief must include:
    - At least one paragraph on the background and significance of the topic
    - At least one paragraph detailing the innovation and its applications
    - One to two original figures (do not copy figures from source material;
      create your own illustrations to describe the main news/innovation)
  Suggested sources: The Scientist, ASBMB Today, or popular press publications
  covering recent life sciences breakthroughs.

  Option B – 3D Protein Model:
  Select a protein structure from the Protein Data Bank (PDB), print it using
  3D printing technology, and submit the model along with a written description
  of the protein and its physiological function.

  Option C – Topoisomerase Demonstration:
  Develop a classroom demonstration showing how topoisomerase functions in
  relieving DNA supercoiling through cutting and rejoining DNA strands.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
EXAMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The course includes three 50-minute term exams administered during class time
and one comprehensive 3-hour final examination given during finals week.

  Exam 1: Covers Lectures 1–10 (Biochemistry through Translation)
  Exam 2: Covers Lectures 11–17 (Cell Biology through Genomes)
  Exam 3: Covers Lectures 19–28 (Cell Trafficking through Cell Imaging)
  Final:  Cumulative, covering Lectures 1–36

There will be no lecture or recitation on exam days.

  26. Cancer 2
  27. Visualizing Life—Dyes and Stains

  32. Infectious Disease, Viruses, and Bacteria
  33. Bacteria and Antibiotic Resistance
  34. Viruses and Anti-viral Resistance
  35. Reproductive Cloning and Embryonic Stem Cells
  36. In the News
